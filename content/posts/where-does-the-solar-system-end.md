Ask ten people where the solar system ends and most will say "Pluto." Pluto isn't even close. Depending on your definition, the true edge is somewhere between 120 and **200,000** astronomical units away — a disagreement spanning three orders of magnitude. Here's why.

## Definition 1: Where the Sun's wind dies — the heliopause

The Sun constantly exhales a stream of charged particles called the solar wind. This wind inflates a bubble around the solar system — the heliosphere. Eventually it slams into the thin gas between the stars and stops. That boundary, the **heliopause**, sits at roughly 120 AU.

This definition has one enormous advantage: we've *been there*. Voyager 1 crossed the heliopause in August 2012 and Voyager 2 followed in November 2018, both registering the moment as a sudden jump in plasma density. By this definition, two human-made machines have already left the solar system.

## Definition 2: Where the Sun's gravity gives up — the Oort Cloud

Gravity doesn't have an edge like a wind bubble does, but there's a practical limit: the distance where a passing star can tug an object away from the Sun more strongly than the Sun holds onto it. That's the outer boundary of the **Oort Cloud** — a vast, theorized shell of icy bodies that begins around 2,000 AU and may extend to 100,000–200,000 AU, nearly halfway to the next star.

By this definition, Voyager 1 — humanity's farthest machine at ~165 AU — has barely stepped off the porch. It won't exit the Oort Cloud for tens of thousands of years.

## Definition 3: The neighbourhood of worlds — the Kuiper Belt

If you only count regions with substantial known objects, the solar system's "main territory" runs out past the Kuiper Belt, the donut of icy bodies (including Pluto) stretching from Neptune at 30 AU to roughly 50 AU. This is the answer planetary geologists tend to picture — the realm of worlds you could land on.

## So which is it?

All three, honestly — "solar system" isn't one thing:

- **The wind ends** at ~120 AU (the heliopause) — crossed by both Voyagers.
- **The worlds end** at ~50 AU (the Kuiper Belt's outer edge).
- **The gravity ends** at up to ~200,000 AU (the Oort Cloud) — about 3 light-years.

The most striking consequence: when people say "Voyager 1 left the solar system," they mean the wind definition. By the gravity definition, no human object will leave the solar system until roughly the year 30,000 AD.
