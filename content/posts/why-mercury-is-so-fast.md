Open our [live solar system map](/solar-system), press play at one month per second, and you'll notice something immediately: Mercury whips around like a moth at a lamp, Earth strolls, and Neptune just… sits there. Run it for a minute and Mercury completes orbit after orbit while Neptune crawls through a sliver of its path.

This isn't an artistic choice. It's the orrery solving real orbital mechanics — and the pattern it reveals is one of the oldest laws in physics.

## Kepler's discovery

In 1619, Johannes Kepler found that a planet's year and its distance from the Sun are locked together by a precise rule: **the square of the orbital period equals the cube of the orbital distance** (in years and AU). No exceptions, no adjustable knobs.

Try it on Mercury. Its distance is 0.39 AU. Cube that: 0.059. Take the square root: 0.24 years — about 88 days. Exactly Mercury's year. Now Neptune: 30 AU cubed is 27,000; the square root is ~164 years. Also exactly right.

## But *why* does closeness mean speed?

Two reasons stack on top of each other:

**1. The Sun pulls harder up close.** Gravity weakens with the square of distance. Mercury feels the Sun's pull about 6,000 times more strongly than Neptune does. To avoid falling in, a closer planet must move faster — orbiting is really just falling sideways quickly enough to keep missing.

**2. The track is shorter.** Mercury's orbit is about 77× shorter in circumference than Neptune's. Faster runner, shorter track: that's how 88 days versus 165 years happens.

The actual speeds: Mercury averages about **47 km/s**, Earth about 30 km/s, Neptune only **5.4 km/s**. Neptune hasn't completed even two full orbits since its discovery in 1846; it finished its first in 2011.

## One more wrinkle: planets speed up and slow down

Orbits are ellipses, not circles, and Kepler's second law says a planet moves fastest at its closest point to the Sun and slowest at its farthest. Mercury, with the most stretched orbit of the eight planets, varies between 39 and 59 km/s within a single 88-day year. On the orrery you can catch this if you watch closely — Mercury visibly hurries through one side of its orbit.

## Why this matters beyond trivia

This same law is why spacecraft trajectories work at all. When engineers plot a route to Mars, they're choosing an ellipse whose timing Kepler could have computed by hand. The Voyager probes' grand tour of the outer planets was a four-century-old equation, executed perfectly.
