There's a football-field-sized laboratory flying 400 kilometres over your head at 28,000 km/h, and you can see it tonight with your bare eyes. The International Space Station is the third-brightest object in the sky after the Sun and Moon — most people just don't know when to look up.

## Why you can see it at all

The ISS makes no light of its own. What you see is sunlight glinting off its acre of solar panels. That's also why timing matters: you can only see it when *you* are in darkness but the *station* is still in sunlight — which happens in the hour or two after sunset and before sunrise.

## What it looks like

A bright, steady, white-gold point of light gliding across the sky — about as bright as Venus on a good pass. It does not blink. If it blinks, you're watching an airplane. A full horizon-to-horizon pass takes about six minutes, and the station is often visibly *fast* in a way that startles first-time spotters.

## How to find a pass

1. **Check the live position** — our homepage tracker shows where the ISS is right now, updating every ten seconds.
2. **Get pass predictions for your exact location** — NASA's free "Spot the Station" service emails or texts you a few hours before a good pass, with the direction to face and how high it will climb. Heavens-Above and the ISS Detector app do the same.
3. **Prefer passes above 40° elevation** — predictions list a maximum height in degrees (90° is straight up). Low passes hug the horizon and get lost in haze; high ones are unmissable.

## Spotting tips that actually matter

- **Be outside two minutes early.** The station appears exactly on schedule — orbital mechanics doesn't run late.
- **Look for it rising in the west.** The ISS always travels roughly west to east, because it was launched that direction to borrow Earth's spin.
- **Watch it vanish mid-sky.** On evening passes, the station often flies into Earth's shadow and fades out like a dimming ember — you're literally watching orbital sunset happen to someone else.
- **Wave.** Seven people are usually aboard. They've seen your city from up there.

## The part that sticks with you

The ISS orbits Earth every 92 minutes — the crew sees 16 sunrises a day. The glint of light crossing your sky will cross an entire ocean within minutes of leaving your view. Once you've seen it for yourself, the live tracker on our homepage stops being a curiosity and becomes a kind of clock: the only clock whose hands are people.
